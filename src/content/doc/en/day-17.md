## Palantir: danger and competence

We talked about Palantir as a **pattern**, not a meme:

- **Wide infiltration:** the business model pushes into as many organisations as possible (public and private). The bigger the graph, the more useful it becomes.
- **Data on thousands (and millions) of people:** you do not need phone spyware to worry; integrating systems that already hold identity, filings, relationships, and movement is enough.
- **Database mapping:** what it does **very well** is joining silos, modelling entities and relationships, and making a map visible that nobody held in their head before. That is operational power; it is also concentrated power.

The table question was not “is it legal?” but “**who controls the map when a third party owns the map?**”. For KM0 the contrast is local: Cloud and mail in the EU, public price, no selling the profile as the product.

## Harari again: AI already writes better

We returned to Harari because [day 14](/en/doc/day-14/) left the thesis open: civilisation runs on **bureaucracy** (law, forms, registries), and AI can operate inside that language at scale.

At the Casino the nuance was more psychological:

- Models **understand (or simulate understanding) words** more consistently than a tired human: tone, templates, exceptions, gaps.
- Writing “better than us” is not only style: it is **closing procedures**, drafting reports, filling holes, and proposing the next step.
- “Hacking” bureaucracy here does not mean cracking a server: it means **finding cracks in the text** (ambiguity, exceptions, deadlines, poorly defined forms) and using them with precision.

If bureaucracy is a word game, whoever masters the language of the game has the edge. That is frightening and, at the same time, explains why tools like Palantir gain ground: they do not only store; they **interpret and connect**.
